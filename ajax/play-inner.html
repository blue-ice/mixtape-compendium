Editor's Notes: Tracks

Our mysterious mix master, Jamie, is quite obviously a rock fan- specifically a fan of several popular rock bands of the 1960s-1980s. Jamie's inclusion of 3 Bob Dylan tracks, 3 Guns N' Roses tracks, 3 Grateful Dead tracks, and 2 Fleetwood Mac tracks shows an obvious dedication to the bands of the era. Nevertheless, the mixtape is not entirely rock: the inclusion of ABC by The Jackson 5 shows an interest in the Billboard #1 soul song as well. The tracks Alone (Blues Traveler) and Copperline (James Taylor) also highlight an interest in the changing genre of alternative rock in the early 1990s.

The juxtaposition on this mixtape between ABC (The Jackson 5) and One in A Million (Guns N' Roses) is strange indeed. Michael Jackson (of the Jackson 5) worked tirelessly to perform his music despite overt racism; he also often spoke out against racism publicly in his later years.[1] Yet Jamie also included the racist and homophobic song that got Guns N'Roses Ku Klux Klan endorsement and a dumping from an AIDS benefit concert:[2] One in A Million. This track features the lyrics "Police and niggers / That's right / Get outta my way" and "Immigrants and faggots / They make no sense to me / They come to our country / And think they'll / Do as they please / Like start some mini Iran / Or spread some / Fucking disease".[3] It's hard to say what's exactly going through Jamie's mind- perhaps our mixtape composer simply enjoys the musicality of the songs and disregards the lyrics.

Editor's Notes: Mixtape

Paradoxically, although this mixtape appears to be hand-recorded from physical albums, the mixtape composer didn't manage to squeeze the last track, Alone, all of the way onto the album. Some sort of miscalculation, perhaps? Laziness?

The rest of the mixtape, however, is meticulously put together, with fine recording techniques and very little wow or flutter from the recording device. The music reproduction quality, overall, is stellar, and there seems to have been little degredation of the mixtape since it was first recorded. This recording date must be after 1991, which was when the most modern song on the compilation, Copperline, by James Taylor, was released.
